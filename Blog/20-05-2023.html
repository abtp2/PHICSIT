Artificial Intelligence & Fate of the Future White Coats

Sanjay Vivekanandan

---- Author

Date :15-05-2023

Read Time :6 min

It is no doubt that artificial intelligence has been finding its way into every possible work field imaginable, and yes, it has dug its nose into the revered domain of health care too. However, will AI really replace our doctors? Will it be our ally on its course? Stay tuned to find out.Future with AI:   There has been a paradigm shift within the medical field with the integration of AI, and the same trend is predicted to continue for the foreseeable future. The health care market for AI is increasing at a rate of 40% and is expected to reach $15.6 billion by 2030. AI has the potential to improve patient outcomes by 30% to 40% while reducing treatment costs by up to 50%, as per research conducted by the firm Frost & Sullivan.   From patient self-service chat bots to computer-aided detection (CAD) systems for diagnosis and image data analysis to identify microbes in drug discovery, AI is already increasing convenience and efficiency, reducing costs and errors, and making it easier for more patients to receive the health care they need.   So, let's head straight into the benefits that AI could provide in the medical industry:

1. Diagnosis and Treatment: AI can help doctors diagnose complications rapidly and more accurately. AI can analyse with ease vast amounts of data and provide insights into complex medical issues that mankind cannot process. AI algorithms have been developed to detect and analyse medical images, such as X-rays, CT scans, MRIs, etc., to identify abnormalities left out by human radiologists.   Evidence : Srinivasu et al. (2021) used an AI algorithm to perform automated segmentation of CT scan images to identify abnormalities in the human liver. It was observed that the proposed approach outperformed the classical methodology in the majority of the cases with an accuracy of 78%.

2. Predictive analytics:By analysing the medical history of patients, AI algorithms function to predict the likelihood of developing extreme conditions, such as myocardial infarction or diabetes, and also suggest appropriate preventive measures.   Evidence : A study published in the journal Diabetes Care found that an AI algorithm was able to accurately predict the onset of type 2 diabetes with a success rate of 94%.

3. Personalised Medicine: AI models have already been developed to help doctors devise a personalised treatment plan for patients. AI identifies the best course of treatment for a particular condition, taking into account factors such as the patient’s age, sex, and medical history.   Evidence : In an experimental study conducted by the University of North Carolina Lineberger Comprehensive Cancer Centre, an AI model was able to provide actionable treatment recommendations for nearly 96% of the patients diagnosed with pre-terminal-stage cancer.

4. Administrative Tasks: AI can be used as an administrative agent, as AI-powered chatbots are widely being implemented to answer patient questions and schedule appointments, reducing the workload for administrative staff.   Evidence : A study published in the journal ‘NPJ Digital Medicine’ found that an AI chatbot was able to appropriately respond to and resolve patient queries in 77% of cases. The chatbot also managed to handle a high volume of patient queries, which ended up saving loads of time for the administrative staff. Now, before we come to a conclusion, there are two sides to every coin, so it's only fair to look into the negatives of AI in healthcare.

THE CLOUD IN THE SILVER LINING :

* Bias: A major challenge faced by AI is bias in the data used to train AI algorithms. This leads to inaccurate or unfair predictions and exacerbates inequalities.   * Privacy and security concerns: AI algorithms rely heavily on sensitive patient data, raising concerns about privacy and security. There is a breachable risk of exposure to personal health information.   * Limited human interaction: The appearance of AI in healthcare limits human interaction between patients and healthcare professionals. Resulting in less empathetic care and missed opportunities for identifying important nonverbal cues.   * Lack of Accountability: It is almost next to impossible to hold AI accountable for any negative outcome in patient treatment.   * Technical limitations: Although AI has shown promise in many areas of healthcare, it is not a panacea (a solution for all difficulties or diseases). There exist technical limitations, such as the inability of AI algorithms to interpret complex medical information and a lack of transparency in arriving at the solution.   After weighing the pros and cons cautiously, the time has finally come to answer the question, "Will AI replace our doctors?"   Put simply, the answer is no. (It’s funny seeing your shocked expressions.) Let me explain why AI cannot replace professionals:   No matter the benefits provided, AI heavily lacks the ability to provide the empathy and compassion that often form the very core of building a strong doctor-patient relationship. Furthermore, human doctors possess a level of clinical judgement and decision-making ability that AI models are still incapable of.   With all that being said, it does not necessarily mean that AI in healthcare is unwelcome; the role of doctors is likely to evolve exponentially as AI becomes more prevalent in healthcare.

SUMMARY :

To summarise, doctors and AI integrated together can help bring about a better future rather than relying on either one. Doctors need to upskill to work alongside AI systems, interpret the data they provide, and make decisions based on that data.   BASELINE :

AI carries certain abilities that cannot be achieved by mankind, and similarly, AI can never replace the doctor-patient relationship, empathy, and clinical judgement offered by us humans. In order to have a better future, both must go hand in hand.   "Human plus AI is far more powerful than either one alone." — Kai-Fu Lee, technology executive and venture capitalist.
